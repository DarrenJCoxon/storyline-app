# Book DNA Guide

The 12 Book DNA stages are shared across all three NF pipelines. Every NF book — prescriptive, narrative, or how-to — starts here. The DNA captures the decisions that are pipeline-agnostic: who the reader is, what the book promises, who the author is to write it, and what category it sits in commercially.

This guide explains why each stage matters and what a strong answer looks like.

---

## Stage 1 — Category & Market Positioning (`dna-category`)

**Why it matters:** Publishers, booksellers, and readers use category to decide whether to pick your book up. If you're in the wrong category, you're invisible to your intended reader and confusing to everyone else. Category also determines your comparables (comps), your competitive field, and which pipeline fits your structure.

**What a strong answer looks like:**
- A primary category that maps to a real Amazon/bookstore shelf
- A sub-category that narrows it usefully (not just "Business" but "Business > Leadership > First-Time Managers")
- Awareness of who else is in that sub-category and whether there's room for another book

**Common mistake:** Choosing the category you want rather than the category the book fits. A memoir is not automatically "inspirational non-fiction" just because the writer finds it inspiring.

---

## Stage 2 — Reader Avatar (`dna-reader`)

**Why it matters:** Every word in a non-fiction book is a choice made for or against a specific reader. Vague reader = vague book. The reader avatar is not a demographic — it's a specific person with a specific problem, a specific level of existing knowledge, and a specific reason to read your book instead of another one.

**What a strong answer looks like:**
- Age, role, situation (not as demographic data but as context for their problem)
- The problem they have that your book solves
- What they've already tried that hasn't worked
- What they're afraid of / what makes them push back
- How they read (in depth? skimming? looking for permission? looking for a method?)

**Common mistake:** "My reader is anyone who wants to improve themselves." That reader doesn't exist. The reader who picks up *Atomic Habits* is specifically someone who has tried and failed to build habits and wants to understand why.

---

## Stage 3 — Reader Transformation (`dna-transform`)

**Why it matters:** A non-fiction book is a promise: the reader who finishes this book will be different from the reader who started it. That difference — the transformation — is the book's value proposition. If you can't state it clearly, the book doesn't know what it's trying to do.

**What a strong answer looks like:**
- A **before state** (what the reader believes, fears, or can't do when they start)
- An **after state** (what they believe, can do, or no longer fear when they finish)
- A transformation that's specific enough to be meaningful and general enough to apply to your full target reader base

**Common mistake:** Confusing information delivery with transformation. "The reader will learn about habit psychology" is information. "The reader will understand why their previous attempts failed and will have a system that makes future success likely" is transformation.

---

## Stage 4 — The One Big Idea (`dna-idea`)

**Why it matters:** Great non-fiction books are remembered for one idea — even when they contain dozens. That one idea is what reviewers quote, what readers tell friends, what gets the book recommended years later. If you can't state the book's one big idea in a sentence, the book has a clarity problem.

**What a strong answer looks like:**
- One sentence. Not a paragraph.
- Counterintuitive or at least not obvious — if the reader already believes it, why do they need the book?
- Specific enough to be tested — someone should be able to disagree with it

**Common mistake:** A topic masquerading as an idea. "This book is about the power of habits" is a topic. "The most reliable way to change behaviour is to change identity before changing actions" is an idea.

---

## Stage 5 — Author Angle & Authority (`dna-author`)

**Why it matters:** Non-fiction readers ask "why should I believe you?" The author angle is the answer. It's not a biography — it's the specific credential, experience, or perspective that makes you the right person to write this book. Weak authority kills trust before the first chapter.

**What a strong answer looks like:**
- The specific reason you have insight on this topic that others don't
- Whether your authority is professional (expert practitioner), experiential (you lived it), or investigative (you reported it thoroughly)
- Acknowledgment of the limits of your authority — where you're relying on sources rather than personal experience

**Common mistake:** Listing credentials instead of explaining relevance. "I have a PhD in psychology" tells the reader your credential. "I've spent 15 years studying why people fail to form habits despite wanting to, and I've tested interventions with 3,000 participants" tells them why the credential matters for this book.

---

## Stage 6 — Core Promise & Subtitle Engineering (`dna-promise`)

**Why it matters:** The subtitle is the book's primary sales tool. It appears on the cover, in search results, in social shares, and in every bookshop description. It must communicate the core promise in a handful of words. The promise must be deliverable — if the book can't keep it, the book breaks trust.

**What a strong answer looks like:**
- A working subtitle that states the transformation or insight the book delivers
- A core promise in one sentence that the subtitle encodes
- A reality check: does the book actually deliver this promise? (The critique layer will test this at NF-08)

**Common mistake:** A subtitle that's a description rather than a promise. "An Exploration of Habit Formation" describes. "How Small Changes Lead to Remarkable Results" promises.

---

## Stage 7 — Comps Deep Dive (`dna-comps`)

**Why it matters:** Comps tell publishers where your book fits commercially and tell readers "if you liked X, you'll like this." Bad comps signal that the writer hasn't read in their category. Good comps position the book in a competitive field and make the gap your book fills visible.

**What a strong answer looks like:**
- 2–4 comparable books published in the last 5 years (for commercial positioning)
- 1–2 "legacy comps" — older books that established the genre your book inhabits
- For each comp: what it does well, where it falls short, and how your book is adjacent but distinct
- A clear statement of what gap your book fills that the comps don't

**Common mistake:** Choosing comps that are too famous ("my book is like *Sapiens*") or too obscure ("my book is like a 2003 academic monograph"). Both signals are wrong. Aim for successful but not intimidating.

---

## Stage 8 — Voice & Tone (`dna-voice`)

**Why it matters:** Voice is the personality on the page. Tone is how that personality shifts by context. A non-fiction book with no voice is a report. Voice is what makes a reader feel they're in conversation with a specific, interesting person rather than reading a document.

**What a strong answer looks like:**
- 3–5 voice adjectives that describe the writer's natural register (e.g. "precise, dry, curious, skeptical, generous")
- A tonal range: what's the warmest this book gets? What's the most rigorous?
- 1–2 writer references — non-fiction authors whose voice direction is closest to yours (not whose content, whose *style*)

**Common mistake:** Aspiring to a voice the writer can't sustain. If you're naturally dry and precise but you decide to write with "warmth and vulnerability," you'll produce uncomfortable hedging. Write the voice you can actually hold for 80,000 words.

---

## Stage 9 — Evidence Philosophy (`dna-evidence`)

**Why it matters:** Non-fiction books make claims. Claims require evidence. The evidence philosophy determines what type of evidence the book leans on and how it treats uncertainty. This is an intellectual commitment that affects every chapter.

**What a strong answer looks like:**
- Primary evidence type: peer-reviewed research / primary sources / case studies / personal experience / investigative reporting — pick one as the anchor
- How you handle contradictory evidence (ignore it / acknowledge and explain / present both sides)
- How you signal epistemic uncertainty ("the research suggests" vs "the research proves")
- How Pipeline B's evidence standard relates to Pipeline A's — Pipeline B often relies more on primary source reporting; Pipeline A on peer-reviewed meta-analysis

**Common mistake:** Claiming scientific authority for anecdote, or hiding evidence uncertainty behind confident prose. Readers who catch either will not trust anything else in the book.

---

## Stage 10 — Commercial Model (`dna-commercial`)

**Why it matters:** The commercial model determines what a successful book looks like financially and strategically for the writer. A book can be a stand-alone product, a lead generator, a course companion, a speaking platform, or a community anchor. The model should influence structure: a lead generator needs a CTA; a course companion needs clear chapter-to-module mapping.

**What a strong answer looks like:**
- Primary commercial model: standalone / lead gen / course companion / platform / series anchor
- Whether this is the first of a series or standalone
- What success looks like in 12 months (sales threshold, audience built, speaking gigs, or specific business outcome)

**Common mistake:** Writing the book without knowing the commercial model, then trying to retrofit one after. A book written as a standalone is structured differently from a book written as course pre-work.

---

## Stage 11 — Working Title Pressure-Test (`dna-title`)

**Why it matters:** The working title will probably change before publication. That's fine. But it still needs to be pressure-tested now, because a bad working title shapes bad development decisions — it signals the wrong reader, promises the wrong thing, or traps the book in the wrong position.

**What a strong answer looks like:**
- 3–5 working title options
- For each: does it signal the right reader? Does it hint at the transformation? Does it stand out from the comps?
- A "north star" title — the one that feels closest, even if it's not perfect
- Awareness of what a future publisher will want to change and why

**Common mistake:** Falling in love with a title too early. The title should serve the book, not the other way around. If the book's development reveals that the title is wrong, change the title.

---

## Stage 12 — Book DNA Consolidation (`dna-consolidate`)

**Why it matters:** All 12 stages have to be coherent with each other. A crisp reader avatar should match the evidence philosophy. The core promise should map to the transformation. The commercial model should align with the author angle. This stage checks for internal coherence before the pipeline-specific stages begin.

**What a strong answer looks like:**
- A confirmed pipeline selection (A, B, or C) with rationale
- Any tensions between stages identified and resolved (e.g. voice that doesn't match comps; evidence philosophy that doesn't match reader avatar expectations)
- A one-paragraph "book positioning statement" that integrates: reader, problem, transformation, authority, evidence, pipeline

**Common mistake:** Treating consolidation as a summary rather than a pressure-test. If stages 1–11 don't cohere, the problems surface here — and they need to be resolved before entering the pipeline, not deferred to the pipeline critique.
