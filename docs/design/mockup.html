Chapter Two

The next morning, the refectory was a different world.

Three hundred boys at the long oak tables, the noise of cutlery and china and the low roar of voices that never quite became words. Sun coming in hard through the high windows. The smell of fried bread and weak tea. Everything too bright, loud, and public after the icy quiet of the computer room.

Theo sat at the end of one of the long tables, alone. He always sat alone. A bowl of cornflakes in front of him. A jug of milk he had not poured. A glass of orange juice he had not touched.

The boys further up the table ate without looking at him. That was the usual arrangement. It was also, this morning, a small mercy. Theo did not feel like being looked at.

*  *  *

He had not slept. Not properly. He had lain in his narrow bed and listened to the breathing of the other boys — Patterson, deep and steady; Crane, who talked sometimes in his sleep about cricket — and stared at the ceiling and thought about his father.

Good — Theo is solid. A boy who notices everything and says almost nothing is exactly the kind of protagonist this story needs.

Now let's build the people around him. Each supporting character should make Theo's central question harder to answer, not easier.

Start with the person closest to him at school. Who does Theo actually talk to?
There's Oliver Arden — a year above, seems to know more than he lets on. And Iris, his father's former student, now at the school for unclear reasons.
Two good instincts. Let me push on both.

Oliver — "knows more than he lets on" is promising, but it's a function not a character. What does Oliver want from Theo specifically?

Iris is more interesting — she carries the father's world into the school. Threat, ally, or does she not know herself yet?
