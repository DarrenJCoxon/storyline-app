# Novel Writer — Beat-by-Beat Guide

Quick reference for the 15 Save the Cat beats. Use this when you need deeper explanation of a specific beat during planning.

## Act 1 — Setup (~25% of story)

### Beat 1: Opening Image
**Purpose:** Snapshot of protagonist's world BEFORE disruption. Sets tone, mood, and "before" state.

**What to capture:**
- A specific visual moment (not just "a city")
- Protagonist in their ordinary world
- Shows character without explaining them
- Tone of the overall story

**Common mistakes:**
- Starting too early (before the real story)
- Starting too late (past the inciting incident)
- Opening with action that has no emotional grounding
- Opening with a dream/flashback (audiences hate this)

**Example:** "A cramped apartment at 3am, the glow of a laptop screen showing spreadsheets, a man who's been awake for 36 hours slumping forward onto the keyboard."

---

### Beat 2: Setup
**Purpose:** Introduce protagonist's everyday world. Establish their flaw in action. Theme is "hidden" here.

**What to capture:**
- Who is the protagonist when nothing is happening
- What do they believe about themselves and the world
- Key relationships and their dynamic
- Show the flaw in action, not stated

**The flaw must be VISIBLE:**
- A character who can't trust would be seen making safe choices
- A character with a core lie of "I'm not worthy" would be seen seeking external validation

**Theme "hidden" means:**
- The story's central question is visible in how characters behave
- The B story (when introduced) often states the theme explicitly

---

### Beat 3: Catalyst
**Purpose:** The moment that disrupts everything. Forces the protagonist to make a decision.

**What to capture:**
- What happens (specific event)
- How it specifically affects THIS protagonist
- Why they can't just ignore it

**The catalyst must be LIFE-CHANGING:**
- Not a minor inconvenience
- Creates a "hole" in their world they feel compelled to fill
- Forces a decision (even if the decision is to do nothing, that IS a decision)

**Timing:** Around page 12-15 (in a 100-page Act 1)

**External vs. Internal catalyst:**
- External: bomb goes off, diagnosis, someone disappears
- Internal: health diagnosis, emotional trigger, personal failure
- Both work — what matters is it disrupts everything

---

### Beat 4: Debate
**Purpose:** The protagonist asks "Should I go? Should I act? Can I win?" They hesitate, prepare, doubt.

**What to capture:**
- What is the protagonist debating?
- What are the possible paths?
- How does the flaw show up in their hesitation?

**The flaw must SABOTAGE the decision:**
- A character who can't trust would see all the ways this could go wrong
- A character with "I'm not worthy" would doubt their ability to succeed

**This is NOT:**
- Just planning with no internal conflict
- The protagonist already knowing what to do

**Timing:** Page 12-25

---

### Beat 5: Break Into Two
**Purpose:** The protagonist COMMITS. They cross the threshold into the new world.

**What to capture:**
- What threshold do they cross?
- What is the "false reality" — what do they think the new world will be like?
- Is this a REAL CHOICE with consequences?

**Must be a conscious choice:**
- Not "they got kidnapped" — they chose to stay and fight
- Not "circumstances forced them" — they decided despite fear

**The break reveals the WANT:**
- What the protagonist thinks they want on a surface level
- They believe achieving this will make everything okay

**Genre variants:**
- Standard: physical departure
- Puppy Love: first romantic encounter
- Buddy: buddy assigns mission or they commit together
- Whydunit: decides to investigate despite danger

---

## Act 2 Part 1 — Response (~25% of story)

### Beat 6: B Story
**Purpose:** The "thesis" story begins. Often a love story, mentorship, or buddy dynamic. Carries the THEME.

**What to capture:**
- Who is the B story character?
- What is their want/need?
- How does their arc mirror or contrast the protagonist's?

**The theme statement:**
- Often stated explicitly by the B story character to the protagonist
- "You can't help others until you help yourself"
- "Sometimes the right thing is the scary thing"

**B story rules:**
- Starts here even when the A plot is quiet
- Must have its own arc (setup → complication → resolution)
- Must be resolved in Act 3

**Common mistake:** B story doesn't start until late Act 2 — it must begin at Beat 6

---

### Beat 7: Fun and Games
**Purpose:** This is the PROMISE OF THE PREMISE. The movie you were sold. Each scene explores what this story is really about.

**What to capture:**
- What are the key Fun and Games scenes?
- What is the promise of your premise?
- How does each scene explore a different angle of the central conflict?

**Promise of the premise examples:**
- Mystery: every clue reveals a new angle
- Romance: every encounter deepens the connection
- Thriller: every obstacle escalates the danger

**Pacing note:**
- Vary intensity — not all scenes same tone
- This is where the protagonist is ACHIEVING their want
- Every win reveals the flaw more

**Timing:** Pages 25-50

---

## The Midpoint (~50%)

### Beat 8: Midpoint
**Purpose:** FALSE VICTORY or FALSE DEFEAT. Stakes are raised. The protagonist moves from reactive to proactive.

**Two types:**

**False Victory:**
- Protagonist achieves their want
- Discovers it creates a new, worse problem
- They got what they thought they wanted — and it's terrible

**False Defeat:**
- Things look catastrophic
- They've lost everything
- But in the darkness they learn something crucial that changes everything

**What to capture:**
- Which type is your midpoint?
- What specifically happens?
- What is the flip or reveal?
- How do stakes raise?

**Stakes escalation:**
- Before midpoint: what was at risk?
- After midpoint: what is NOW at risk?
- The gap must be significant

**Timing:** Around page 50-55 (middle of the book)

---

## Act 2 Part 2 — Confrontation (~25% of story)

### Beat 9: Bad Guys Close In
**Purpose:** External pressures mount. The protagonist deals with midpoint fallout. Internal doubt grows.

**What to capture:**
- What pressures mount?
- Are they specific to this story (not generic)?
- How does external failure mirror internal flaw?

**The antagonist is not the only threat:**
- The protagonist's flaw should be "attacking" them
- Every attempt to solve the external problem makes the internal problem worse

**Timing:** Pages 55-75

---

### Beat 10: All Is Lost
**Purpose:** Right before the lowest point. The "whiff of death" — something or someone precious is lost.

**What to capture:**
- What is the whiff of death?
- Is it literal death, or symbolic (relationship, hope, belief)?
- How does it connect to the protagonist's flaw or the antagonist's victory?

**The whiff of death must be GENUINE:**
- Can't be reversed immediately
- Must hurt
- Must create the despair needed for the Dark Night of the Soul

**The Dark Night of the Soul:**
- The emotional aftermath
- Where do they almost give up?
- What do they confront about themselves?

**Timing:** Around page 75

---

### Beat 11: Black Moment
**Purpose:** The protagonist has hit bottom. Everything looks darkest. Almost ready to quit.

**What to capture:**
- What is the lowest point?
- How is this darker than All Is Lost?
- What gives them one last reason to try?

**Must be the LOWEST point:**
- Darker than the midpoint false defeat
- Darker than All Is Lost
- The protagonist genuinely believes they've lost

**The "spark" that prevents hopelessness:**
- B story character says something
- A memory surfaces
- Someone believes in them
- Something they've learned gives them an idea

**Timing:** Pages 75-85

---

## Act 3 — Confrontation (~25% of story)

### Beat 12: Break Into Three / Second Doorway
**Purpose:** The protagonist finds the solution. Not through luck — through what they've LEARNED.

**What to capture:**
- What is the second doorway?
- How is it EARNED through growth?
- How does it force them to confront their core lie?

**The second doorway must be IMPOSSIBLE in Act 1:**
- The protagonist couldn't have taken this path at the start
- They've become someone different
- Their transformation makes this choice possible

**Not a lucky break:**
- The protagonist sees a new way
- They've grown enough to recognize it
- They choose to fight because they've changed

---

### Beat 13: Finale
**Purpose:** The protagonist proves they've changed. External battle and internal revelation happen together. Flaw is overcome. Need is met.

**What to capture:**
- What does the climax look like?
- How does the protagonist USE their transformation to win?
- What is the self-revelation?

**Three types of climax:**

**External Battle:** Physical confrontation, protagonist uses new understanding to win

**Internal Revelation:** The protagonist chooses correctly because they've grown — this IS the battle

**Both Simultaneous:** The external fight IS the internal revelation — winning requires the change

**Self-revelation:**
- Not "I won" but "I understand what I've become"
- The moment they see the truth about themselves
- Need is met because they accept the truth

**New equilibrium:**
- The "after" image
- World is different, protagonist fits differently in it

---

### Beat 14: Final Image
**Purpose:** Mirror or inversion of the Opening Image. Proof the protagonist has changed.

**What to capture:**
- What is the final image?
- How does it contrast with the opening?
- Is the protagonist visible in the change?

**Examples:**
- Opening: man slumped at 3am over spreadsheets
- Final: man at sunset, laptop closed, walking away
- Opening: woman alone in crowded room
- Final: woman holding someone's hand in that same room

---

### Beat 15: End Credits
**Purpose:** "And then they lived." Shows the new equilibrium. Brief, satisfying.

**For novels:** The final beat that confirms the change has stuck

**What to capture:**
- How does the protagonist appear living in their new state?
- Is it brief, not over-explained?
- Does it confirm the transformation is real?
